Starting in Bug Bounty: The Overwhelming Journey to My Underwhelming First Bug

Aug 11, 2023
8 min read

This year I set a goal for myself to buckle down on bug bounty and find a valid bug. TL;DR - I did meet my goal (sort of) and as the title says - it’s underwhelming… but I’m more motivated now than ever and I hope this motivates you as well if you’re new to bug bounty!

Disclaimer: This isn’t advice on what to do, per se, or “how to be successful” in bug bounty - there are many opinions about those things. This is just my (lack of) experience.

But first, a quick backstory

I’ve worked in cybersecurity for several years now and got both of my degrees in Information Technology. Most of my education and job focus has been on the defensive side of things, but being the curious person I am I’ve spent a considerable amount of time on TryHackMe and HackTheBox because I have always been fascinated with hacking.

Occassionally I’ll participate in CTF’s and I’ve even finished in the top 10 on several smaller events of about 150ish participants! Naturally the desire to “do bigger things” and potentially earn some extra money led me to bug bounty. My YouTube history became loaded with videos from NahamSec, STÖK, and several others so I had to give it a shot.

I’m pretty self-aware and realize that I’m not a 1337 h4ck3r, but I’ve got some general understanding of vulns and how to exploit them. I use linux (not arch btw), dabble in python, can read code relatively well… This will be fairly easy right??

Meh… not so much :)

Hunting bugs for the first time

Full disclosure - I still struggle with these challenges, but I hope that you (and I!) can learn from my mistakes.

Challenge #1: Picking a target

This probably deserves a topic on its own, so I’ll be brief. Choosing a target to hunt was like finding a needle in a haystack. Only there are many haystacks filled with many needles all around you and all you have to do is open your eyes and grab one. So I picked one and started hacking. About an hour later I picked another, and another, and another…

Challenge #2: Start hacking

Long story short - I started some recon, launched Burp and started going through the motions of looking for bugs. Only “there were none” I thought, so I moved on to my next target. Rinse, repeat. I got stuck in an infinite loop of whack-a-mole jumping from program to program only to come up empty-handed.

At this point I’m thinking that I’m just an imposter. Was I wrong for assuming that because I have some knowledge and skills that I should be able to find bugs? Do I actually possess the knowledge and skills that I think I do??

Well, I wasn’t necessarily wrong - my approach was wrong. Also, I do have at least some skills, so that wasn’t right either. So why is this so hard?

That’s hacking for you. The bugs are there it’s just not always evident, nor is it always easy.

Changing my mindset

Around this time the Critical Thinking Bug Bounty Podcast was launched. I looked for bug bounty podcasts in the weeks before CTBB launched, but there wasn’t really much out there. The timing could not have been more perfect. Justin and Joel really have something special with that podcast, so if you’re not listening you are missing out! Seriously!

One episode that sticks out in my mind is where they are talking about this very subject. Essentially you are trying to hack a company that literally pays a team of people to prevent hackers from hacking them as their full-time job. Just let that sink in… the odds are not in your favor as a hacker!

With that, I decided to step back and reflect on what I was doing, as well as address my “imposter syndrome”. First, I had to remind myself that even though I’m not a seasoned hacker, I do in fact know several bug classes and how to exploit them in the general sense. There’s always room for improvement, but the fundamentals are there. Heck even the seasoned hackers have imposter syndrome, so why should I be discouraged? I’m not alone in struggling to hack.

Second, I have other hobbies that are not related to tech and I was not spending much (if any) time doing them. I was solely focused on finding a bug and it was driving me insane. My wife reminded me of this and suggested that I take a break and do something else. She was right (like always! :P) and I’m grateful to have such a supportive wife.

There’s more I could say about this part of my journey, but just know that finding your identity in something outside of hacking really helps with this.

Changing my approach

With my head back on straight, it was time to restrategize. In addition to CTBB, one talk I had listened to came to mind: zseano’s talk at NahamCon (from 2021 I think?) . What it boils down to is rather than getting stuck in the infinite loop of program hopping, pick one target and stick to it for the long game. Why? Because there are bugs to be found everywhere, you just need to take the time to find them. You can’t very well do that if you’re bouncing from program to program.

This is solid advice! I decided that I would not leave a program until I had uncovered every piece of the application inside and out.

The results

I spent about two months on a single program and learned a lot about how it behaved, what functionality my profile had, how it was gathering data, etc. Then one day I saw something! I found an API key for the application itself. After some fiddling around and reading the API docs I was able to validate that the key belonged to a consumer for a third-party integration with the app. It had limited functionality, but ultimately I believed it was worth reporting so I submitted it as a P4.

Having never submitted a bug report, this was another hurdle for me. The fear of a rejected report. I didn’t know what to expect from the triage team or the program itself. A few days later I was heading out of town with my wife when I got an email from the triage team. I glanced at it quickly and saw that it had been changed from a P4 to a P1!!!!

Say whaaaaaaattttt!!??!? My first bug submission was a P1!? I was not expecting that at all!!

…but wait, there’s more…

I didn’t get to read the entire message because I was driving, so what I didn’t see was that it was also a duplicate… and it was also marked as NA….

I was disappointed, but I just laughed it off. I got over my fear of submitting my first bug and was ready to move on. Over the next few weeks I submitted a couple more bugs to the program and they were not dupes, but they were marked NA as there wasn’t really any impact.

Ultimately I regrouped and ended up settling on a mobile application. I’ve not had much experience with mobile hacking, but it was interesting to me and personally I’m more likely to stick to something that I find interesting. By the way, that is another key component to being successful in bug bounty. Hack on stuff that you use and that you like!

The app I was hacking on was not something that I personally used, but I found the process fun and enjoyable. I only spent a couple of weeks on it before I actually found something interesting (at least I thought).

I submitted my report as a P4 finding and it got accepted, but it was dropped to a P5…

I know, underwhelming right!? I wasn’t kidding :)

Like all hackers I don’t want to submit P5’s, and it’s not something that you should submit anyway. These companies are looking for value in the reports and P5’s just don’t do it.

The point of all of this

My story is not unique in any way. Everyone struggles when they first start out and some people spend a year or more hunting for bugs and give up because they don’t find anything! Considering my rocky journey so far, the one thing I’ve learned is this: persistence and patience go a long way. Even though I’ve not found anything significant up to this point, I’m better than I was yesterday and that means more to me personally.

Rather than having a goals focused on finding X number of bugs, or getting paid, my goals now are simply to get better every day, continue learning and have fun. The bugs will come. Even if I fail it’s not like I’m going to quit.

Also, if you’re new to all of this and you are going to submit bugs make sure you can demonstrate some sort of impact! This has been a hard lesson to learn for me, but after a few NA’s I feel like I better understand the process in some sense. Proving that something is valid is one thing - proving that it has impact is totally different.

If you made it this far, thank you for reading and I hope you enjoyed it!

GLHF!
